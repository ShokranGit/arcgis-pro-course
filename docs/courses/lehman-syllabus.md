## Course Description

The intent of this course is to introduce students to the basic principles and applications of Geographic Information Science (GISc) and Geographic Information Systems (GIS). The topics covered in this course include but are not limited to geographic data collection, spatial data models, spatial querying, analysis and modeling, geodesy and map projections, and cartography and geovisualization. Lectures provide the basic knowledge and theory needed to understand and use GIS. Laboratory exercises and the GIS project are designed to familiarize students with GIS software and ground the theoretical classroom discussions in practical applications. All students will have access to computers with ArcGIS Pro Desktop software. Lectures and laboratory work combined aim to develop spatial thinking and analysis skills. The semester-long GIS project is designed to provide students the opportunity to apply their GIS skills with originality and complexity in a topic of their choice.

## Learning Objectives

Students successfully completing this course will be able to:

- Demonstrate an understanding of the fundamentals of concepts, theories, data models, data structures in GIS/GISc
- Demonstrate an understanding of the applications of GIS/GISc in various fields to solve real-world problems.
- Implement common techniques and software tools for collecting, storing, querying, analyzing, and visualizing geographic data.
- Compare the strengths, weaknesses, and assumptions built into GIS functions.
- Create maps and understand the basic cartographic principles.
- Design a GIS project using the skills acquired throughout the course and address the problems defined in the project.

## GIS Project

The GIS project is a semester-long project to address a real-world problem of geographic relevance using GIS. It represents a chance for students to apply newly acquired GIS skills to an area of their interest. To ensure the successful development of the project, three milestones are set throughout the semester (instructions and grading rubric for each milestone will be provided in the class):

- **Project proposal:** A document of 2-3 pages (single-space) stating a real-world geographic problem, describing the background, and identifying necessary data and a general outline of the methods intended to answer the question.
- **Project methods:** A revised version of the proposal based on the feedback provided by the instructor, with the addition of a methods section.
- **Project final paper:** A 10 to 15-page (single-space) document describing the whole project, including results and findings and their interpretation. The project final paper should include revisions based on the feedback from the project proposal and methods submissions.

**No late submissions will be accepted.**

In project workshop sessions and office hours, each group/student will receive feedback on their project and make necessary revisions before submitting the next milestone (i.e., proposal, methods, and final paper).

*For undergraduate students, the project will be carried out in a group of 3 students. Graduate students are required to work individually on the project.*
